A visual guide for non-musicians

How does a program know
what key a song is in?

From raw sound waves to a musical key — no music theory required. Here's exactly what happens inside the algorithm, step by step.

Step 01
AUDIO

We start with a sound wave

When you record audio, a microphone measures how much air pressure is hitting it, thousands of times per second. The result is just a long list of numbers — how loud the sound is at each tiny moment in time.

Amplitude over time — ~44,000 measurements per second
0.023 0.187 0.412 0.631 0.589 0.301 -0.082 -0.445 -0.721 ... 44,100 per second
💡

This raw data tells us how loud the sound is at each moment. But it says nothing about which notes are playing. To find notes, we need to transform it.

Step 02
FFT

Slice & transform into a frequency recipe

We chop the audio into short overlapping windows (~50ms each). For each window, the Fast Fourier Transform answers: "what ingredients (frequencies) make up this sound, and how much of each?" Think of it like identifying spices by smell.

Before — one 50ms window
After — frequency spectrum
100 Hz 440 Hz 880 Hz 4 kHz
🎸

The spike at 440 Hz means there's a strong note at 440 vibrations per second — that's the musical note A4. The spike at 880 Hz is the same note, one octave higher.

Step 03
CHROMA

Collapse into 12 note buckets

There are only 12 unique note names (C, C#, D … B). All octaves of the same note get merged into one bucket — a 440 Hz A and an 880 Hz A both count as "A". The result is a chroma frame: a 12-number snapshot of which notes are present.

Chroma frame — energy per note (one 50ms window)
📦

In this example, C, E, and G are dominant — that's the "recipe" for a C major chord. We get one of these 12-number snapshots for every 50ms of audio.

Step 04
PROFILES

The 24 reference fingerprints

Music researchers asked thousands of people "does this note sound stable in this key?" and averaged their answers. The result is two reference patterns — one for major, one for minor. Each is 12 numbers: a "fingerprint" of what that type of key typically sounds like.

Krumhansl–Kessler Profiles (from music perception research, 1982)
Major Profile
Root, 3rd & 5th are strongest
Minor Profile
Root strong, 3rd lower than major
🔢

We rotate these 12-number patterns to represent each of the 12 possible root notes. Major + Minor × 12 roots = 24 reference fingerprints total.

Step 05
COMPARE

Match the chroma to every fingerprint

For each of the 24 keys, we rotate the chroma frame to line up with that key's root note, then measure how similar its shape is to the major/minor profile. The closest match wins that frame.

Rotation aligns the chroma to each key's root. Try C — the chroma frame has C, E, G peaks which align perfectly with the major profile. Try F# — terrible match, low score.

Step 06
DECIDE

Every frame votes, the track wins

Steps 3–5 happen for every 50ms window in the whole track. Each frame casts a weighted vote for a key — frames where one note clearly dominates count more. Natural chord transitions (like G→C) also get bonus points. The key with the most total votes wins.

Accumulated vote scores across all frames — example 3-minute track
C Major → Camelot 8B
The algorithm outputs the Camelot notation used by DJs — so you can instantly find harmonically compatible tracks to mix.
